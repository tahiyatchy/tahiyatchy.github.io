Evolution of Research Ethics Timeline

Explore the historical development of ethical standards in human subjects research:

  • Pre-1900s
  • 1900-1945
  • 1946-1978
  • 1979-Present

Early Research Practices (Pre-1900s)

Key Characteristics:

  • Limited formal ethical guidelines
  • Research often conducted without explicit consent
  • Focus on scientific advancement over participant welfare

Notable Events: Early vaccine trials, anthropological studies often conducted without informed consent protocols.

Emerging Concerns (1900-1945)

Key Developments:

  • First formal research ethics guidelines begin to emerge
  • Growing recognition of need for participant protection
  • Nuremberg Code established in response to WWII atrocities

Landmark: The 1932 Tuskegee Syphilis Study begins (later becomes a major ethics case study).

Regulatory Foundation (1946-1978)

Major Milestones:

  • 1947: Nuremberg Code establishes informed consent principle
  • 1964: Declaration of Helsinki provides international guidelines
  • 1974: National Research Act establishes IRB system in US
  • 1978: Belmont Report outlines three core ethical principles

Impact: Foundation of modern research ethics regulations and oversight systems.

Modern Era (1979-Present)

Contemporary Developments:

  • 1991: Common Rule establishes uniform US federal policy
  • 1996: HIPAA introduces health information privacy protections
  • 2000s: Growing focus on genetic privacy and data security
  • 2010s: Emphasis on community-engaged and participatory research

Current Focus: Digital ethics, AI in research, global research ethics standards.

Research Ethics Glossary

Click on terms to learn key concepts and definitions in research ethics:

Informed Consent

Informed Consent is the process by which a research participant voluntarily confirms their willingness to participate in research, after having been informed of all aspects of the research that are relevant to their decision.

Key Elements:
  • Disclosure of relevant information
  • Participant comprehension
  • Voluntary participation
  • Ongoing consent process

IRB (Institutional Review Board)

IRB (Institutional Review Board) is a committee that reviews, approves, and monitors biomedical and behavioral research involving humans to protect their rights and welfare.

IRB Review Levels:
  • Exempt: Minimal risk research
  • Expedited: Minimal risk, quick review
  • Full Board: More than minimal risk

Vulnerable Populations

Vulnerable Populations are groups of participants who require special protections in research due to increased risk of coercion or diminished capacity for informed consent.

Examples Include:
  • Children and minors
  • Prisoners
  • Cognitively impaired individuals
  • Economically disadvantaged persons
  • Educational subordinates (students)

Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest exists when professional judgment concerning a primary interest may be unduly influenced by a secondary interest.

  • Financial conflicts
  • Personal relationships
  • Academic pressures
  • Institutional loyalties

IRB Regulations and Review

Institutional Review Boards ensure that research involving human subjects is conducted ethically.

Exempt Review

Minimal risk research that qualifies for exemption from full IRB review.

Expedited Review

Minimal risk research that doesn't qualify for exemption but can be reviewed by one IRB member.

Full Board Review

Research involving more than minimal risk requires review by the full IRB committee.
